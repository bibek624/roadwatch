# Treatment signatures — what a recently-treated pavement looks like

A pavement that's been treated (sealed, overlaid, mill-and-overlaid, reconstructed) often LOOKS different from untreated aging asphalt. Recognizing treatment signatures matters because:

1. **A recently-treated surface should grade Good or Satisfactory** even if the underlying pavement was previously cracked. The treatment reset the clock.
2. **A treatment that's failing prematurely** signals an underlying structural issue — grade according to what's visible (you may still see Failed if reflection cracks are coming through a thin overlay).

## Treatment menu (asphalt pavement preservation)

Listed roughly from cheapest / lightest to most invasive:

### 1. Crack seal

**What it is.** Hot-applied asphalt rubber poured into individual cracks to seal them against water infiltration. Targeted at specific cracks, not the whole surface.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Black bituminous lines tracing each treated crack
- Lines often 5-15 mm wide, slightly raised above the surface
- Often visible as DARK lines on a faded GREY pavement (treated pavement was already aged)
- Pattern of lines mimics the underlying crack pattern (single longitudinal cracks, network of transverse cracks, etc.)

**Service life:** 2-5 years [8, 10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 71-85 (Sat tier with minor cracking).

**Grading implication:** A pavement showing fresh crack-seal lines OVER an otherwise-OK surface is **Sat or Fair** depending on the underlying pavement. Don't penalize the existence of crack seal — it's a sign of good maintenance. But also don't grade Good — the underlying pavement is aging enough to need sealing.

### 2. Fog seal

**What it is.** Diluted asphalt emulsion sprayed across the entire surface to rejuvenate aged binder.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Uniformly DARK surface (much darker than aged grey asphalt)
- Slightly glossy when fresh
- No texture change — surface looks like new but smooth like the original
- Often markings are obliterated (re-painted afterward) → lane lines may look very fresh

**Service life:** 2-4 years [10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 71-85; aged but structurally sound pavement.

**Grading implication:** A fog-sealed surface is **Sat or Good**. If you see uniform dark asphalt with crisp lane lines — possibly a recent fog seal. The "freshness" of the surface vs. the age of the surrounding street furniture (utility patches, sidewalks) tells you it's a treatment, not new construction.

### 3. Slurry seal

**What it is.** Mixture of fine aggregate, asphalt emulsion, and water spread across the surface as a thin layer. Fills minor surface defects.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Uniform DARK surface, finer-grained texture than typical asphalt
- May show squeegee marks initially (smooth out within weeks)
- Lane markings re-painted after application → bright fresh markings on dark surface
- Distinguishable from fog seal: slurry has visible fine aggregate texture; fog is smoother/glossier

**Service life:** 4-7 years [10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 65-85 (Fair-Sat range).

**Grading implication:** Sat or Good depending on uniformity. If the slurry is uniform across the lane, with crisp markings, grade Sat (Good if very fresh).

### 4. Microsurfacing

**What it is.** Polymer-modified slurry, thicker and tougher than slurry seal. Can fill rutting up to ~40 mm.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Uniform DARK surface, slightly coarser texture than slurry
- ~3/8 inch (10 mm) thick — visible as a slight elevation above original pavement at edges
- Often applied in wheelpaths to fill ruts (visible as wheelpath strips of darker fresh material)
- Crisp re-painted markings

**Service life:** 5-8 years [10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 60-80, with rutting or moderate cracking.

**Grading implication:** Sat. If you see a recently-microsurfaced surface, grade Sat — visible distress is reset.

### 5. Chip seal

**What it is.** Asphalt emulsion sprayed first, then aggregate (chips) spread on top, then rolled. The bound aggregate layer becomes the new wearing surface.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- LIGHT-COLORED surface (the aggregate dominates color, not binder) — markedly lighter than other asphalt treatments
- Coarse, rough TEXTURE — you can see individual chips
- Loose chips initially in gutters / lane edges (sweep-up takes a few days)
- Distinct: no other asphalt treatment produces a light, rough, chunky-textured surface

**Service life:** 5-7 years [10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 55-75 (rural roads, lower-traffic streets).

**Grading implication:** Sat. The surface is rougher than fresh asphalt but it's a fresh wearing surface — not distress. Don't call the rough texture "raveling."

### 6. Cape seal

**What it is.** Chip seal followed by slurry seal — combination treatment.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Resembles slurry on top (smooth dark) but with the underlying chip-seal texture sometimes showing through
- Thicker than either treatment alone

**Service life:** 6-9 years.

**Grading implication:** Sat to Good.

### 7. Thin overlay (Thinlay)

**What it is.** New asphalt layer ~25 mm (1 inch) thick over the existing pavement. Provides a fresh wearing course and minor structural improvement.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Looks like NEW PAVEMENT (uniform black, fresh texture, sharp markings)
- Distinguishable from full reconstruction by: edge transitions visible at curbs / driveways / utility patches (the new layer is sitting on top of the old structure; pre-existing fixtures remain)
- Sometimes visible mat seams between paving lanes

**Service life:** 8-12 years [10].

**Triggers application at:** PCI 55-70 (Fair).

**Grading implication:** Good. A thin overlay applied within the last few years should grade Good. If you see fresh-pavement appearance over an otherwise old-looking street (old curbs, settled manhole patches sitting in the new surface), it's a thin overlay. Don't penalize the manhole settlement on a freshly-overlaid surface — grade the overlay's condition.

### 8. Mill-and-overlay

**What it is.** Top inch or two of old pavement is milled (ground off), then a new asphalt layer is paved on top. Removes the surface distress with the milled material.

**Visual signature when fresh:**
- Identical to thin overlay visually (uniform fresh asphalt, crisp markings)
- Distinguishable by milled-pavement striations sometimes visible at edges before overlay (rare to see in a finished state)

**Service life:** 12-18 years.

**Triggers application at:** PCI 50-70 (Fair, beginning Poor).

**Grading implication:** Good.

### 9. Hot in-place recycling (HIR)

**What it is.** Existing asphalt is heated, scarified, and re-laid in place with optional addition of new binder/aggregate. Recycles the existing material.

**Visual signature:** Resembles fresh thin-overlay; not commonly distinguishable visually.

**Grading implication:** Good or Sat.

### 10. Full-depth reclamation (FDR) / reconstruction

**What it is.** The full asphalt layer (and sometimes the base) is removed and replaced. Effectively a new pavement.

**Visual signature:**
- Fresh PAVEMENT throughout — no pre-existing distress
- Often involves fresh curb-and-gutter, ADA-compliant ramps, fresh markings
- Edge transitions are clean (driveway aprons may be re-poured)
- The whole street looks recently constructed

**Service life:** 18-25+ years.

**Grading implication:** Good. You can grade with confidence at the upper bound.

## How to identify "this segment was treated recently"

Tells:

1. **Markings are fresh** but the rest of the street (curbs, sidewalks, signage) is older.
2. **Surface is darker than its neighbors** on the same street.
3. **Treatment edge visible** at intersections — sometimes you can see where the treatment ended (a tonal transition partway down a block).
4. **Utility patches visible THROUGH the treatment** as elevated bumps (treatment was applied around existing utility cuts).

When you see these tells, **grade the treated surface** based on the treatment's freshness, not the underlying distress. A 6-month-old microsurfacing on a 20-year-old pavement is Sat — the surface is what cars are riding on right now.

## How to identify "this treatment is failing prematurely"

A treatment that's failing shows signature mix-and-match: fresh-looking surface PLUS new cracking that doesn't match the treatment age.

- **Reflection cracking through thin overlay:** transverse cracks at regular intervals propagating through an otherwise-fresh asphalt layer. Implies underlying joint or rigid-layer movement.
- **Slurry seal already raveling:** fresh dark surface but with aggregate loss showing. Indicates poor application (cool weather, dust on substrate, premature traffic).
- **Microsurfacing rutting back:** wheelpaths sinking on a freshly-laid microsurface. Indicates the underlying rutting wasn't fully addressed and is propagating up.

In these cases, **grade by what's visible.** If the failure is severe enough to dominate the pavement visually, the segment is back in Phase 2 / 3 territory regardless of the recent treatment.
