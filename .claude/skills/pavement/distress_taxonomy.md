# Distress taxonomy — what each defect actually looks like

This is your visual dictionary. When you describe a finding's rationale, name the distress(es) you saw using the vocabulary below. Each entry's *Visual cues* tells you how to identify it; *Confusers* tells you what NOT to mistake for it; *Severity* tells you which severity level (low / medium / high) you are seeing.

Severity in this skill aligns with ASTM D6433 [2] severity bands. Tier in [`tier_rubric.md`](tier_rubric.md) integrates these severities into the 5-tier scale.

## 1. Crack — longitudinal

**Definition.** Linear fracture running PARALLEL to the pavement centerline / direction of travel.

**Mechanism.** Three causes converge: (a) joint construction at lane-edge tends to compact poorly and cracks early; (b) top-down fatigue from repeated tensile stress in wheelpaths; (c) reflection from underlying-layer discontinuities. [4]

**Visual cues.**
- Single or a few parallel lines along travel direction
- Often along the white edge stripe (construction-joint cracks) or in wheelpaths (fatigue origin)
- Linear, not interconnected (interconnection → alligator or block)

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: < 10 mm width, no spalling
- Medium: 10–19 mm width, or low-severity with sparse spalling
- High: > 19 mm width, or any width with raveled/spalled edges

**Confusers.**
- Painted lane line / edge stripe — paint has crisp uniform edges and consistent width; cracks have ragged irregular edges and variable width
- Construction joint between original lanes — joint is straight, thin, sometimes sealed; that's normal, not distress unless cracked

## 2. Crack — transverse

**Definition.** Linear fracture running PERPENDICULAR to the centerline. Runs side-to-side across the lane.

**Mechanism.** Thermal contraction of the asphalt + binder hardening (oxidation) over time. Cold climates produce dense networks of evenly-spaced transverse cracks; hot climates show fewer. Reflective transverse cracks propagate up from joints in underlying rigid pavement. [4]

**Visual cues.**
- Straight or slightly curved crossing of the lane
- Often regularly spaced (every 4-15 m)
- Width may be sealed (black bituminous fill) or open

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: < 10 mm, no spalling
- Medium: 10-19 mm, or any width with light spalling
- High: > 19 mm, severely spalled

**Confusers.**
- Crosswalk stripes — paint, parallel multiples, intentional
- Stop bars — paint, single thick line
- Pavement joint between adjacent paving days — straight, thin

## 3. Crack — alligator (fatigue)

**Definition.** Interconnected cracks forming MANY-SIDED, sharp-angled pieces resembling reptile skin. Concentrated in wheelpaths.

**Mechanism.** Structural fatigue: the pavement structure is too thin or weakened (by stripping, water infiltration, lost subgrade support) to carry the traffic load. Fatigue cracks initiate at the bottom of the asphalt layer and propagate upward. [5]

**Visual cues.**
- **Located in wheelpaths** (this is the diagnostic — alligator is load-induced)
- Pieces are SHARP-ANGLED and IRREGULAR, not rectangular
- Pieces are SMALL (< 0.3 m on a side typically)
- Severe: pieces visibly loose, may rock; pumping or water staining at crack network

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: fine cracks, NO spalling, no pumping
- Medium: cracks interconnected forming alligator pattern; slight spalling
- High: alligator with separated/loose pieces, possible base failure visible

**Confusers vs Block cracking.** Alligator pieces are **small + irregular + in wheelpaths + load-induced**. Block cracking is **larger + rectangular + everywhere (including non-traffic areas) + thermal-induced**. If you see interconnected cracks but pieces are 0.3-3 m rectangles spread across full lane width including parking → that's block, not alligator.

## 4. Crack — block

**Definition.** Interconnected cracks dividing the pavement into RECTANGULAR pieces 0.3-3 m on a side (per ASTM D6433 [2]; alternative ranges in literature are 1-100 ft²).

**Mechanism.** Thermal expansion-contraction cycles + binder oxidation/aging. NOT load-related. Common in low-traffic areas (parking, residential) of older asphalt. Indicates the binder has aged past its flexible service life.

**Visual cues.**
- Pattern covers BROAD areas — full lane width, parking aprons, often outside wheelpaths
- Pieces are CLEARLY RECTANGULAR
- Pieces are LARGER than alligator pieces
- Often accompanied by surface oxidation (greying)

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: cracks < 10 mm, no spalling
- Medium: cracks 10-19 mm, or any width with sparse spalling
- High: cracks > 19 mm, blocks moving

**Confusers.** Alligator (smaller, in wheelpaths, sharp-angled, load-induced). Driveway / utility-cut joints (single straight lines, not interconnected pattern).

## 5. Pothole

**Definition.** A bowl-shaped hole that PENETRATES THROUGH the asphalt layer to the base course, with sharp edges and near-vertical sides near the surface.

**Mechanism.** Final-stage failure of fatigue cracking: alligator pieces dislodge under traffic, leaving a hole. Water infiltration accelerates expansion.

**Visual cues.**
- BOWL-SHAPED with abrupt sharp edges
- Visible base course material (gravel / brown / different color from surrounding asphalt) at the bottom
- Often water-filled or stained
- Distinct from depressions (which are smooth surface settlement, no exposed base)

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: < 30 cm (1 ft) diameter and < 2.5 cm (1 in) deep
- Medium: 30-60 cm diameter or 2.5-5 cm deep
- High: > 60 cm diameter or > 5 cm deep

**Confusers.** Manhole cover (round, intact metal, intentional, no exposed base). Drain inlet (rectangular grate). Utility patch (rectangular shape with edge seams; depth < 1 cm; surface flush). Shadow on uniform pavement (no actual depth, edges fade gradually).

## 6. Patch failure

**Definition.** Any area of pavement that has been REPLACED with new material (utility cut, pothole repair, lane reconstruction) AND is exhibiting deterioration at the patch.

**Mechanism.** Patches age differently than surrounding pavement: the patch material may be different mix, the bond at edges fails first, traffic stress concentrates at patch boundaries.

**Visual cues.**
- Rectangular tonal contrast vs surrounding pavement (patches almost always rectangular; circular = pothole repair)
- **Failure indicators**: edge separation (cracks tracing the patch perimeter), edges raised or sunken (settling), patch material itself cracking or alligator-cracking, ruts at patch boundaries

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: patch in good condition, edges flush, no spalling
- Medium: patch has moderate deterioration (edge cracking, light settlement)
- High: patch is severely deteriorated (significant cracking within patch, large settlement, edges spalled)

**Confusers.** Tonal differences from differential weathering / oxidation are NOT patches — they're aging. Pavement-marking obliteration (where a marking was painted over) is not a patch. To call something a patch you should see a defined rectangular boundary indicating cut/replace work.

## 7. Raveling (and weathering)

**Definition.** Progressive loss of aggregate particles from the pavement surface, exposing binder and creating a worn / pitted texture. Includes related "weathering and raveling" (combined ASTM Distress 19) for general age-related surface deterioration.

**Mechanism.** Loss of adhesion between aggregate and binder due to: binder oxidation/hardening with age, dust coating preventing bond, inadequate compaction, mechanical abrasion (studded tires, plows, tracked vehicles).

**Visual cues.**
- Surface texture rougher than new — exposed aggregate visible
- Loose stones / aggregate accumulating in gutters or off the lane
- Color shift: surface looks lighter / greyer than fresh asphalt
- Severe: actual gaps where aggregate used to be

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: slight aggregate loss, surface still mostly intact
- Medium: moderate loss, surface visibly deteriorating
- High: severe loss, large bare patches, structural concern

**Confusers.** Bleeding (binder UP not aggregate gone — surface looks SHINY not pitted). Polished aggregate (aggregate intact but smooth). Sand or dust deposits (loose material that washes off).

## 8. Edge break / edge cracking

**Definition.** Cracking, breaking, or crumbling of the pavement within ~0.5 m (1.5 ft) of the pavement edge.

**Mechanism.** Inadequate edge support (no shoulder or weak shoulder) + traffic loading at the edge + water infiltration weakening the edge subgrade.

**Visual cues.**
- Cracks paralleling the edge
- Edge crumbling — material missing, irregular pavement edge profile
- May connect to alligator pattern in adjacent wheelpath

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: fine edge cracks, minimal spalling
- Medium: edge cracks with some spalling, edge still mostly intact
- High: severe cracking and spalling, pavement edge has lost material or is separating

**Confusers.** Construction joint near edge (intentional, straight). Edge raveling without breaking (surface loss without underlying break — call that raveling).

## 9. Rutting

**Definition.** Longitudinal depressions in the wheelpaths where the pavement has permanently deformed under repeated loading.

**Mechanism.** Three contributors: (a) inadequate initial compaction (pavement densifies under traffic), (b) subgrade settlement (foundation moves), (c) plastic deformation of weak surface mix in hot weather.

**Visual cues.**
- LONGITUDINAL depressions in the wheelpath (this is diagnostic — rutting is wheelpath-specific)
- Pavement uplift visible alongside rut (shoving)
- Water pools in the ruts after rain
- Severe: ruts pull vehicles toward the rut; hydroplaning hazard

**Severity (ASTM D6433):**
- Low: < 6 mm (1/4 in) depth
- Medium: 6-13 mm (1/4-1/2 in)
- High: > 13 mm (> 1/2 in)

**Confusers.** Depressions (small / non-wheelpath / circular). Corrugation (transverse ripples not longitudinal grooves). Shadows in low light look like ruts but lack actual depth.

## 10. Utility cut

**Definition.** A specific patch type: rectangular cut-and-patch placed by a utility crew (water, gas, electric, telecom) to access subsurface infrastructure.

**Mechanism.** Excavation cut, repair, refill with asphalt patch. Common in dense urban networks where utilities run under streets.

**Visual cues.**
- Distinct rectangular boundary, often 1-3 m on a side
- Tonal contrast (often darker than surrounding pavement when fresh; lighter when older)
- Often centered on a manhole or near a curb
- Edges may be visible as faint joint lines

**Severity (treated as a patch — see Patch failure).**
- Low: flush, intact, no edge cracking
- Medium: edge cracking, minor settlement
- High: depressed significantly, alligator within or around the cut

**Confusers.** Pothole repairs (same shape but smaller, often circular or amorphous). Lane / parking-bay paint patches (single tonal area but no boundary cracks).

## A note on the 19-distress vs 10-distress mapping

ASTM D6433-21 defines 19 asphalt distress types [2]. We collapse them into 10 for practical street-level grading because several ASTM types are not reliably distinguishable at Mapillary photo resolution:

- ASTM "Bleeding" + "Flushing" + "Exudation" → all surface-binder-up phenomena. Under our taxonomy: included in `raveling` (the inverse / weathering family) — we don't track binder-up separately because it's both rare and visually subtle in our imagery.
- ASTM "Bumps and Sags" + "Depressions" + "Corrugation" + "Shoving" → vertical-displacement family. We treat these implicitly through the rubric ("rutting" + tier disqualifiers like "wave"); not in our 10.
- ASTM "Slippage Cracking" — a specific crescent-shaped failure; rare; we'd describe it under longitudinal in rationale, not a separate type.
- ASTM "Polished Aggregate" — a wear/friction issue not a structural distress; not relevant to our 5-tier rubric.
- ASTM "Reflection Cracking" — we describe these with their geometry (longitudinal or transverse) in rationale; the *cause* (reflection from rigid base) is engineering interpretation, not what the camera sees.

Our 10 cover the FHWA / ASTM-named types that are **visually distinguishable from a 360° street-level photograph** and **load-bearing for the tier decision**.
